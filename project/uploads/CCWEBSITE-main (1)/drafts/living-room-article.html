We Reorganized the Living Room This Week

By Skylar Belt  ·  Founder, ConnectEd Circles  ·  Parent  ·  Asheville, NC
[ PHOTO: The lamb hat in the bin. Natural light. Not styled. ]

We reorganized the living room this week.

That’s the neutral version.

We pulled out the stuffed animals. The chewies. The jar lids that used to be their favorite basket ever — better than any toy we bought them. Them holding, admiring, chewing on mason jar lids while they struggled to stay upright in front of the basket.

Scout screamed in my ear as I tried to pull their lamb hat over their head. They squirmed. Finally, with a pull, I got it over their ears. Barely. It covered the tops of their ears, which I was counting as a win.

See, it still fits, I said to Anja behind me, who was also trying to pull a pilot cap over Remi’s head.

Suddenly Scout’s eyelids started to shrink. Their nose wrinkled and before I could think —

ACHOO.

Scout sneezed. The hat popped off their ears and sat on top of their head, barely hanging on. In one small instant their face went from confusion to pure bliss — a single toothy grin, hands flapping at their sides.

I sighed. Bowed my head in defeat.

It’s too small, I finally said.

Anja wasn’t ready for the voice of reason role either. She came over, pulled the hat over Scout’s ears, tied a careful bow under it. Scout’s cheeks squeezed to the tops of their eyes. The hat held for a beat — just passing over the lobes of their ears — when a jolt of humor burst through them and they threw their head back in that big toothy grin. The hat flew off and hit what could have been the ground but was a small hill of chewies, shakers, and sensory parts.

Anja looked at me.

You’re right. They’re too big.

We put the hat in a basket. Storage, or Goodwill.

I just can’t believe they’re turning one soon, one of us said.

I’m not ready.

Me neither.

It wasn’t just the hat. We looked around and saw a space built for babies who were beginning to sit — who liked to admire the magical items we carefully pulled from a basket. Not two babies crawling, pulling up, dumping out baskets. Glancing at a pile and climbing over it to get to a windowsill and pull themselves up.

I think we need to redo the room, Anja said. Her shoulders slumped — not defeat, just acceptance.

They need different things now.

Well. Where do we start?

[ PHOTO: The cleared floor. Wide angle from the doorway. No kids in frame. ]

We took out most of the toys. Organized them into bags. Put them away — not forever, but not for all the time. We geared the room towards motor, towards movement. Things to climb on, pull up on. Pillows to crawl over. A basket of balls. A handful of selected stuffed animals. A bookshelf. A couch for us — and then them — to pull up on. And when they grow tired of moving, we pull out something intentional. Their instruments. A puzzle whose pieces they still use mostly as a snack.

At the end it felt clean. It also felt gutting. The small things that had made them so happy were no longer as relevant. Just as we were getting used to them as small babies, they were ready to begin teetering towards toddlerhood.

We weren’t ready.

Which is probably true of most of their story. The NICU felt like a cold bath — clean and sobering. My body would shiver with fear, sadness, worry, but it never could relax long enough to cry. We weren’t ready for what was being thrown at us then either. But there’s no choice. You try anyway.

We’re going to continue to not be ready for what parenthood throws at us. And still we’ll try.

The moment we plopped Remi and Scout in the middle of the new floor, they sat up frozen. So still, just taking in everything. Their whole world had changed.

Then Remi let out what can only be described as a scream — impeccable, full-bodied. Scout, looking around the same room, let out a slightly softer squawk, no less energized. Then took a deep breath, dropped to hands and knees, and dashed to the TV stand — now completely transformed — and pulled up to look at a single stuffed animal that had been in the room for weeks. And shook with joy as if seeing it for the first time.

[ PHOTO: One of them pulling up. Mid-motion. Slightly blurry is better than posed. ]

We stood just outside the play space, watching. My hand found Anja’s and gave a short, tight squeeze.

That squeeze was Being With. Not fixing. Not narrating. Not performing delight for them so they’d know how to feel. Just: I’m here. I see this. I’m not going anywhere.

Tomorrow they turn one.

My heart feels like it’s suspended in the air and I’m waiting for the drop but it won’t come. It just keeps floating along.

The room is different now. It reflects who they are today. We put the hat in the bin. We cleared the floor. We watched them shake with joy at a stuffed animal they’d seen a hundred times.

That’s the whole thing, right there.
