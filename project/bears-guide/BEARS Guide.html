A Letter to the Teacher

If you're reading this,
someone handed you a stack of bears and a manuscript.

A vertical photo of a hand-written morning note taped to the inside of a classroom door — the handwriting, the tape, the slight curl of the paper.
A note to myself, taped to the inside of the door.

We know. We've been the teacher in that moment — laminating machine still warm, twenty-six children about to walk in, a binder someone promised would "change everything" sitting unopened on the counter.

So before anything else: this guide isn't one more thing. It's a way of doing what you're already doing — greeting, comforting, holding, watching, surviving — with one small object in the room that makes a child's hardest feelings easier to put down somewhere.

We wrote this in the storytelling tradition of Amy Laura Dombro and the clinical tradition of Emotionally Responsive Practice at Bank Street College, founded by Lesley Koplow. The research is here, and it's strong. Wherever we could, we've told it through stories. Children deserve stories. Teachers do too.

Read what helps. Skip what doesn't. The bears will be patient with you.

— Gabriel and Skylar

There's a structural map of this guide at the back, in Appendix A. We put it there on purpose. Your first contact with this guide should be a child, not a color key.

If you only have ten minutes
Take this with you.
  • Choose bears that are 8–12 inches tall, soft, neutral-faced, and as close to identical as possible. Put them on a low shelf children can reach. Let each child choose one and name it.
  • Watch before you talk. When a child is doing something with their bear, sit nearby. Let them tell you — or don't ask.
  • When you do speak, speak about the bear, not the child. "Your bear looks worried" is something a three-year-old can hear. "You look worried" sometimes isn't.
  • Never take a bear away as punishment.
  • When something hard happens in the room, your steadiness is the intervention. The bear helps. You're the engine.

Everything else in this guide is an elaboration of those five things. If you want the why, read on.

Welcome, Teacher

Inside, the classroom was quiet as morning drop-off merged with free play.

A wide shot of morning drop-off — a child in the doorway holding a bear against their shoulder the way you hold an infant. The teacher is mid-crouch. Rain on the window.
Drop-off, a Tuesday in early October.

Outside, a light rain fell. The teacher sang "Hello, Everybody" as children arrived — some running in, some hanging back, some watching from the doorway.

What stood out was what several children were doing with their bears. Mateo hugged his bear tightly, whispering in its ear.

"How is your bear today, Mateo?" the teacher asked.
"I'm keeping her safe," Mateo said.

Across the room, Aliyah was pulling small wooden blocks off the shelf, one by one, and stacking them slowly around her bear. "Tell me about what you're making for your bear," the teacher said. "It's a super-house," Aliyah said, without looking up. "Because he's scared of thunder."

Near the cubbies, Sienna sat on the floor with her bear face-down in her lap. She wasn't doing anything with it — wasn't dressing it, wasn't talking to it — but her hand stayed on its back the whole morning, the way you'd keep a hand on a sleeping baby. The teacher noticed and didn't interrupt.

Three months earlier, Hurricane Helene had unleashed flooding across our region that destroyed homes, businesses, and roads. Adults struggled to make sense of what had happened. Children struggled more quietly. In the months that followed, caregivers did their best to comfort them. Some talked about the flood. Some made simple books about the storm. And some — like the teacher in this classroom — turned to teddy bears.

This guide is for the teacher who saw those three moments and thought, "Something real is happening here." The teacher who greets children every morning and navigates twenty transitions and twenty small griefs and twenty tiny triumphs before lunch. The teacher who already knows, deep down, that play is how children do the hardest work of their lives.

Healing happens in everyday moments — a child whispering to a bear, a teacher gently naming feelings, a family adding a lullaby to the classroom, a child who just holds her bear and is allowed to.

That's what this guide is about.
Part One
Why Bears,
and why now
The research, the tradition, and what a small stuffed animal can do that a worksheet can't.

Part One · Chapter 1

A child is never only in the classroom.

They bring the car ride, the hard goodbye, the sibling that cried all night, the adult that was scared. They bring bodies that remember things their words can't yet say.

Young children process big feelings in the language they have — and the language they have is play. A child who can't say "I'm worried my dad won't come back" can absolutely build a house around a bear and say, "She's scared, so I'm making it strong." The work is the same. The bear just makes it sayable.

Why a bear, specifically

A bear sits at a useful distance from the child. Close enough to hold, far enough to project onto. A doll that looks like a child can be too close — it's them. A puppet asks the child to perform. A bear is just a bear. Its face is neutral. Its fur absorbs. It doesn't ask anything back.

Bears also carry cultural weight. Almost every adult in a child's life had one, lost one, or wished for one. When a teacher hands a child a bear with both hands — slowly, like it matters — the whole ritual says: this is precious, and so are you.

From the classroom
What Jonah taught us.

Jonah was three when his grandmother died. For weeks, he carried his bear to every part of the day — snack, outside, nap. One afternoon he put the bear under a blanket on the reading rug and sat beside it for twenty minutes, not moving. His teacher sat down nearby, not speaking. When he finally looked up, he said, "She's sleeping. But she'll wake up at my house." Then he went back to blocks. That was the work. That was enough.

What the research tells us

The approach in this guide is rooted in Emotionally Responsive Practice, developed at Bank Street College of Education by Lesley Koplow and colleagues. ERP holds that children's social-emotional development is primary, not supplementary — that the adult's attuned presence is the curriculum, and that play is how children metabolize experience.

What the literature on transitional objects, narrative play, and trauma-informed early childhood practice consistently shows:

  • Children who are allowed to externalize distress onto an object recover faster from everyday upsets.
  • Adults who name feelings in the object ("your bear looks tired") land better with dysregulated children than direct naming.
  • Predictable rituals with a chosen object lower cortisol and support the development of self-regulation over time.

None of this is new. Winnicott wrote about transitional objects in 1953. What's new — or newly urgent — is applying it on purpose, in group care, for whole rooms of children navigating an unstable world.

Reflect
What object got you through?

Before you go further: take a minute to remember an object that mattered to you as a child. A blanket. A stuffed animal. A pillow. A stick. What did you do with it? What did the adults in your life do — or fail to do — around it?

Keep that memory nearby. The way you were held around your object is likely the way you'll instinctively hold children around theirs. That's worth knowing.

How it works — the short version.

Guided bear play is not a curriculum. It's a small set of habits woven into what you already do. Here is the whole architecture in one page:

Part One · Chapter 3

Getting your bears ready.

Before the children arrive, you're choosing bears, choosing a shelf, and choosing a ritual. These small decisions shape a whole year.

Choosing the bears

  • Size: 8–12 inches. Big enough to hold with two hands, small enough to carry everywhere.
  • Softness: washable, squeezable, no hard plastic eyes or nose that could come off.
  • Face: neutral. Not smiling too hard, not frowning. The child needs to paint the face.
  • Similarity: as close to identical as you can manage. If two children want "the brown one," you want the answer to be "there are many brown ones, and each one will belong to the child who chooses it."
  • Quantity: one per child, plus 2–3 extras. Extras matter: a bear gets lost, a bear gets wet, a new child arrives in April.

Choosing the shelf

The bears live somewhere children can reach without asking — a low open shelf near the reading corner is ideal. Not in a basket where bears get crushed. Not behind a door where they feel like a reward. Visible, reachable, and unjudged.

The first day ritual

Try this
A ten-minute introduction.
  1. Gather the class on the rug. Have the bears in a soft pile in the middle, not arranged.
  2. Say something like: "These bears live in our classroom this year. Each of you will choose one. Your bear will be yours. It will have a name only you decide."
  3. Go one child at a time — slowly. Let each child approach the pile, pick up a bear, hold it. Ask, "Is this your bear?" If yes, ask the name. If no, no pressure.
  4. Children who can't decide on day one: fine. They can choose on day two, or day five. The bear isn't going anywhere.
  5. When everyone has a bear, say the names back in a circle. Then put the bears on their shelf together. "Goodnight, bears. We'll see you tomorrow."
Watch for
The child who says "I don't want one."

Don't push. Say, "Okay. Your bear is here when you want it." Sometimes the child who refuses on day one is the child who needs it most — and who will quietly tuck one under their arm in week three without announcement. That's a success. Let it be private.

Daily rhythms, a cozy corner, and the small rituals that make the bear real to the child.

Part Two · Chapter 4

Daily rhythms.

The bear doesn't need a "bear time." It needs to be woven into the times you already have — arrival, meeting, transitions, rest, goodbye.

Arrival

As children come in, their bears are on the shelf waiting. Some children grab a bear immediately. Some don't need one until later. Either is fine. You might say — quietly, not to the whole room — "Your bear is here when you want her."

Morning meeting

Bears can join meeting or stay on the shelf. If a child brings a bear, the bear sits in their lap. Once in a while, invite a bear check-in: "How are your bears today?" Children will tell you everything you need to know.

From the classroom
The day the bears were tired.

After a long weekend, five children in a row said their bears were "tired" at morning meeting. The teacher didn't probe. She just said, "A lot of bears are tired today. Let's have a gentle morning." That was the whole lesson plan pivot. Two hours later, at the lunch table, three of those children told her, unprompted, about the loud thunderstorm on Saturday night.

Transitions

Transitions are where bears earn their keep. The walk to the bathroom, the cleanup song, the line to outside — these are the micro-losses that undo children. A bear in hand can hold what words can't: "we're leaving something we were enjoying, and coming back isn't guaranteed in a three-year-old's mind."

Let children carry bears through transitions until the transition is no longer hard. Then, quietly, the bear stays on the shelf more often. Don't force the timeline.

Rest

At nap or quiet time, every bear is welcome. Bears are exceptional co-regulators lying down. A child who won't rest with a blanket will rest with a bear. A child who can't be touched by an adult at rest can almost always be touched by their bear.

Goodbye

At the end of the day, bears go back on the shelf together. Brief ritual: "Goodnight, bears. See you tomorrow." Children who want to take a bear home should not — the bear belongs to the room. (There's one exception in Part Five: the April home-stay, which is a planned event with a purpose.)

Part Two · Chapter 5

The Cozy Corner.

A photo of a low reading corner: a soft rug, one beanbag, a basket of bears, a small shelf of picture books with worn spines. Afternoon light.
The Cozy Corner — small, soft, predictable, and almost never empty.

The Cozy Corner is the physical home of the bear work. It's not a "calm down" spot (which implies punishment) and it's not a reading nook that sometimes has bears. It's a small, predictable, soft place where the bears live, where 1–2 children can be alone together, and where an adult can sit with a child without the whole room watching.

What it needs

  • A defined edge — a rug, a low shelf, a curtain. Children need to know they're in it.
  • Soft floor. Beanbag or cushion. No hard chairs.
  • The bear shelf, visible.
  • 5–8 picture books, rotated. (Part Six has a starting list.)
  • Enough quiet. Not soundproof — just away from the sensory-intense zones (water, blocks, art).

What it doesn't need

  • A timer.
  • A feelings chart with faces. (Charts become performance. Your voice is the chart.)
  • A sign that says "Calm Down Corner."
  • A waiting list.
Try this
Sit in it yourself.

Before the children use it, sit in the Cozy Corner for five minutes with the lights where they'll be, the sounds of the building, a bear in your lap. Is it actually cozy? Is the light harsh? Is there a draft? Can you see the rest of the room from here, or are you cut off? Adjust. You're designing for a small body that is already overwhelmed.

Part Three · Chapter 6

Inviting and containing.

Your two jobs, in any moment of bear play, are to invite feelings that need to come out and to contain feelings that are already out and getting bigger.

Inviting

Most of the time, you're inviting. You do this by:

  • Noticing out loud, briefly, without demand. "Your bear is all wrapped up today."
  • Leaving space. Count to ten in your head before asking a follow-up.
  • Sitting nearby without hovering. Children tell you more when your body is at their level and your eyes are somewhere else.
  • Reading a book that's adjacent to what you're seeing — not the same, just adjacent. A child building a fort around a bear? Read The Napping House at meeting. Let the child find the bridge.

Containing

Sometimes the feeling is already out. The child is crying, or rough with the bear, or escalating. Your job is to keep the feeling survivable — for the child, for the bear (symbolically), and for the room.

Try this
Three containing phrases.
  • "Your bear is safe. I won't let anything hurt your bear." (Even as you gently stop the child from throwing the bear.)
  • "That's a big feeling. It's too big to hold alone. I'm going to sit with you."
  • "Your bear can cry as long as she needs to. I'll stay."

Notice what's not in these: "calm down," "stop," "you're okay." Children who are not okay need us to not lie about it.

Reflect
Where's your edge?

Every teacher has a behavior that pushes them past containing and into reacting. Throwing? Biting? A specific kind of wailing? Name your edge. Tell a colleague. When you know where your edge is, you can ask for help five minutes before you hit it, instead of five minutes after.

Part Three · Chapter 7

When play gets big.

Sometimes children's bear play gets intense. A child "hurts" their bear. A child buries their bear. A child says their bear is dead. A group of children have the bears do something adults find troubling — a funeral, a fight, a hospital where the bear never wakes up.

The first thing to know: big play is usually healthy. Children are putting something difficult into a container they can control. Your instinct to stop it is understandable and often wrong.

When to stay with it

Stay with the play when:

  • The child is engaged, not dissociated. Eyes are present, narrative is coherent (for their age).
  • The bear is the one "hurt," not a real peer.
  • The play has a plot — even a grim one. Plots mean meaning-making.
  • The intensity builds and releases, rather than spiraling flatter and flatter.

When to gently redirect

Redirect when:

  • Another child is frightened. (Your first job is the whole room.)
  • The play has become repetitive and joyless — stuck, not working through.
  • A child is being physically rough in a way that endangers a peer.
  • Your gut says this child is showing you something that should be shared with a family or supervisor. Note it. Tell someone.
Watch for
The flatness signal.

The difference between "working something out" and "stuck" is usually affect. Working-out play has arcs — tension, release, small smiles of mastery, sighs. Stuck play is flat, automatic, and the child looks slightly absent. When you see flatness, come closer. Sit. Take a bear yourself. Say, "My bear is tired. Can your bear come sit with us?" Join the play at a lower register.

A word about referrals

Bear play is not therapy. You are not a therapist. When a child's play keeps circling the same dark place for weeks, or when you see signs that concern you beyond what play can hold, your job is to loop in your director, the family, and — with their consent — a qualified clinician. Write down what you saw. Dates, quotes, context. Your documentation is a gift to whoever comes next.

Part Four
Tending
your own practice
You are the intervention. This chapter is about taking care of the intervention.

Part Four · Chapter 8

Your little self is in the room too.

When a child cries in a way that reaches into you, you are not only feeling for the child. You are feeling with your own three-year-old self, who is still in there somewhere, still a little unmet.

This is not a problem. It's the mechanism. Teachers who can feel their own child-self are teachers who can genuinely feel children. The goal isn't to suppress it — it's to know it, so it doesn't steer the bus without your permission.

Reflect
Three questions to sit with, once.
  1. Who comforted you as a child when you were scared? What did they do?
  2. Was there a moment in your childhood when no one came? What did you do?
  3. Which of the children in your room right now reminds you most of yourself? What does that child need from you that you didn't get?

You don't need answers. You need to have looked.

The bear for you

Some teachers keep a bear of their own. Not to parent-play with children, but as a private signal — a small object on their desk or in their bag that reminds them: my little self is welcome here too. No rules about this. Some teachers find it silly. Some find it steadying. Either is correct.

Part Four · Chapter 9

The Me Check.

Before you walk into the room, and at one natural pause in the day, do this. It takes ninety seconds.

Try this
The Me Check, in full.
  1. Body. Where am I holding tension? (Jaw, shoulders, gut, hips.) Can I let it down one notch?
  2. Breath. Am I breathing into my belly or my chest? Take three belly breaths.
  3. Feeling. What am I carrying in from outside the room? (A hard conversation, a bad commute, a news alert.) Can I name it — even silently — so it's not running the show?
  4. Child. Is there one child I'm already frustrated with, before they've arrived? If yes, picture them at their best. You will meet the version you expect.
  5. Intention. What's one thing I want to be true about my presence today? Steady. Curious. Slow. Warm. Pick one word. Carry it.

The Me Check with a colleague

If you have a teaching partner, do the Me Check together once a week for five minutes. The conversation is short and specific: How's your body today? What are you carrying? Which child is on your mind? What do you want me to know before kids arrive? Teachers who do this don't just feel better. They catch each other's children before the children fall.

You cannot hold children steady from a body that is not steady. The Me Check is not self-care. It is the work.

Bears
across the year
Ten monthly themes and four anytime themes. Not a script — a set of invitations that follow the shape of the year.

Part Five · Introduction

How to use the monthly themes.

Each month of the school year has a theme that matches what children are actually doing developmentally and emotionally in that month. These are not mandatory. They are not a curriculum to cover. They are a shelf of invitations you can pick from when you need one.

Each theme comes with: a developmental note (what's happening in children now), a book suggestion, a bear ritual to try, and one watch-for. Use what serves your room.

· · ·
SeptemberA Safe Place

What's happening: Children are new to the room, new to each other, maybe new to being away from a caregiver at all. Their nervous systems are scanning. Is this place safe? Is this adult safe?

A ritual to try: Each day, end morning meeting with "where our bears live" — a brief, sung or chanted naming of the bear shelf, the Cozy Corner, the cubby. Repetition is the message.

Watch for: The child whose bear never leaves the shelf. Don't push. Sit near the shelf once a day, alone, reading. Let the child join the shelf with you.

OctoberSame & Different

What's happening: Children are noticing each other. Who has the same snack, who has the same shoes, who has the same color bear. Noticing difference is how identity starts.

A ritual to try: A weekly "bear gathering" — bears line up and children describe one way their bear is the same as another child's and one way it's different. Model: "Our bears are both brown. But yours has a red ribbon, and mine has none."

Watch for: The child who insists their bear is "the best." Don't correct. Offer: "Every bear here is someone's best bear."

NovemberTogether & Apart

What's happening: Long weekends, holidays, travel, absences. Children are being separated from and returned to caregivers in new patterns. Some can name it. Most can't.

A ritual to try: Before a break: "Our bears will stay right here. When you come back, they'll be waiting." After a break: a welcome-back meeting where each child says one thing their bear did while they were gone (bears, of course, did nothing — but the story is the point).

Watch for: Regression after breaks. Normal. Don't treat it like a setback; treat it like a re-entry.

DecemberMany Feelings

What's happening: Holidays concentrate feelings — excitement, disappointment, family tension, cold, dark, overstimulation. Children often "have a great week at home" and then fall apart at school on Monday.

A ritual to try: "How is your bear's day?" as a morning check-in. Give children words: sleepy, sparkly, wobbly, heavy, silly, quiet. Feelings don't have to be named in grown-up vocabulary.

Watch for: The child who can only say "good." Sit with them at snack. Let the silence do the work.

JanuaryLost & Found

What's happening: Mid-year. The novelty has worn off and the hardness of winter has arrived. Children have lost things — a routine, a grandparent's visit, a favorite teacher who went on leave. Some losses are new; some are getting bigger in the re-telling.

A ritual to try: A "lost and found" story time. Read one book a week about something lost and then found (Part Six has options). Let children tell their own lost stories if they want. Don't fish.

Watch for: A child who loses their bear frequently. This is usually communication, not carelessness.

FebruaryScared & Safe

What's happening: Fears come up more as children's imaginations expand. Monsters, dark, bad dreams, being alone. This is developmental, not a problem.

A ritual to try: A "what bears are scared of" conversation at a calm moment — never in the middle of fear. Write down what children say. Post it. It will surprise you. The vacuum. Falling. The inside of the microwave.

Watch for: Nightmares showing up in play. Let the play happen. The daylight version is how the nighttime version loses power.

MarchGrowing & Changing

What's happening: Noticeable growth — of bodies, of skills, of new teeth, of readiness. Also: the arrival of ambivalence about growing up. Many four-year-olds want to be babies and grown-ups in the same hour.

A ritual to try: Let children tell you about "when their bear was a baby." The child is almost always talking about themselves.

Watch for: A child who wants to be fed, rocked, or carried more than usual. Meet it without comment. Regression in the service of growth is a real thing.

AprilTeddy Bear Town

What's happening: Children are now a community. The room has its own internal culture, its own vocabulary, its own grudges and loyalties. Time for the bears to have a week of their own.

A ritual to try: Build Teddy Bear Town — a week-long project where the block area, the art area, and the Cozy Corner become a village the bears live in together. Children make houses, a market, a hospital, a library. The bears have a town meeting. Photograph it all.

The home-stay (optional): At the end of the Town week, each child's bear goes home for one night. Send a small journal. The family writes or draws one thing the bear did. The bears return full of stories. This is the single most powerful bear event of the year. Do it with care. Make sure every family can participate; have backup bears for families who cannot.

MayReunion

What's happening: Children are thinking about the end of the year, even if no one has said the word "end" yet. Bodies know.

A ritual to try: "Bears tell the story of our year." Gather at meeting. Pass a bear around. Each child says one thing their bear remembers. You write them down. You'll use this book in June.

Watch for: Anticipatory grief — clinginess, aggression, sudden fears, "babyish" behavior. Name it gently. "Our year is ending. Endings can feel like a lot."

JuneA Good Goodbye

What's happening: The ending is real now. Children feel it. You feel it. Some children will act as if nothing is happening; some will act as if the world is ending. Both are ways of saying goodbye.

A ritual to try: A final bear meeting. Read the book of memories you made in May. Let each child say one thing to their bear. Then — and this matters — the bears stay. They belong to the room. Next year's children will choose them. (A few centers let children take their bear home. Either works, as long as you decide in September, not June.)

Watch for: Your own grief. Teachers who have done this work for a year are saying goodbye to something real. The Me Check is doubly important in June.

FourAnytime Themes

Some themes don't belong to a month — they arrive when the room needs them. Have a small plan ready for each.

  • A new child arrives. A fresh bear on the shelf, waiting. A returning-child ritual: "Welcome. This is our Cozy Corner. This is the bear who's been waiting for you."
  • A hard community event. A storm, a death in a family, a neighborhood scare. Fewer words. More presence. A book. A bear on your lap at meeting.
  • A child is leaving mid-year. A bear ritual of goodbye, scaled to the age. A small drawing from each classmate tucked into a folder for the child to take.
  • The teacher is away. Name the absence in advance. "I will be gone on Thursday. Your bears will be here. Ms. Lena will be here. I will come back on Friday." Repetition beats reassurance.

Part Six · Chapter 11

Phrases to borrow.

When you're tired, your words get smaller. Keep these in your pocket.

For inviting

  • "Your bear seems [tired / worried / busy / quiet] today."
  • "What is your bear doing?" (Not why.)
  • "Tell me about her."
  • "I'll sit here. You don't have to say anything."

For containing

  • "Your bear is safe. I won't let anything hurt your bear."
  • "That's a big feeling. I'll stay with you."
  • "Your bear can cry as long as she needs to."
  • "Let's sit down. The floor is steady."

For transitions

  • "Your bear is coming with us to [the next thing]."
  • "Say 'see you soon' to the blocks. We'll be back after snack."
  • "Bears go on the shelf. We'll say goodnight to them together."

For goodbye

  • "The end is real. Endings can feel like a lot."
  • "Your bear stays here for the next children. You're the one who chose her first."
  • "You were here. We remember you."

Part Six · Chapter 12

A letter home to families.

A suggested letter to send home in the first week of school. Adapt freely. Translate as needed.

Letter template
Dear families,

This year, our classroom has a shelf of teddy bears. Each child will choose one on their first days here, name it, and know it as theirs. The bear will stay at school — it lives in our classroom.

We use the bears to help children with the big work of early childhood: arriving each morning, saying goodbye to you, waiting, resting, feeling angry or scared or tired, and finding their way back to play. When a child is having a hard moment, we might say, "your bear looks worried" — because it's often easier for a young child to hear that than to hear "you look worried."

You may hear your child mention their bear at home. You might hear about what their bear did, felt, lost, or found. This is your child telling you about themselves, at a distance that feels safe to them. You don't need to do anything special. Listen. Ask one gentle question. Let the bear carry the rest.

In April, there will be an optional "Teddy Bear Town" week, and you'll receive more information about a one-night bear home-stay. Until then, if you have questions about the bears — or anything else — we are here.

With warmth,
Your teaching team

Closing

An invitation.

You don't have to be ready. The bears will be patient with you. The children will show you what to do, if you can watch them with soft eyes.

The work in this guide is old work. Teachers have been sitting beside children and small objects for as long as there have been teachers and children. What we've tried to do is give you language for what you already know, and a few specific practices to try when you want them.

Some of what you try will land. Some won't. That's fine. A practice is built from attempts, not from certainty. What matters is that you keep coming back to the room, keep choosing to be steady, keep noticing, keep — when you can — loving your own little self alongside theirs.

We learn through connection. The bear is a small, patient teacher of what connection feels like — for a child who is still learning how to be held, and for a teacher who is remembering.
