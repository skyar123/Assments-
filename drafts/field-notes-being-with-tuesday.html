What Being With Looks Like on a Tuesday

By Skylar Belt · Asheville, NC

It is 4:17 on a Tuesday afternoon and Remi is weeping on the floor of the kitchen because a piece of avocado has touched a piece of cheese.

The avocado and the cheese have, at this point in their short shared history, touched a thousand times. It has never before been an emergency. Today it is an emergency. I know it is an emergency because Remi’s face has gone from surprise to betrayal to full-body grief in under two seconds, and because a noise is coming out of them that I did not think a body that size could produce. The cheese is still on the avocado. I have not moved.

I want to move. I want to separate the foods. I want to wipe down the tray. I want to hand Remi a different snack. I want to narrate the feeling in a competent voice. I want to make this stop.

I do none of these things. I sit down on the floor about three feet from Remi and I do not say anything. My hand finds the floor. I breathe through my nose. I let my shoulders drop because I can feel them up near my ears and that is information Remi does not need right now. I look at Remi. I do not perform looking. I just look.

Remi continues to weep. I continue to sit.

Scout, who has been across the kitchen doing something quiet with a wooden spoon, walks over. Scout sits down next to me. Scout places the wooden spoon carefully on my knee. Scout looks at Remi. The three of us are now on the floor and no one is talking.

This is when I notice that my jaw has unclenched. I had not known my jaw was clenched. Remi’s breathing has shifted from the ragged top-of-the-chest cry to something lower and more regular. Not calm. But rearranging.

I do not fix the avocado. The avocado will be fine.

After about ninety seconds — which is a geologic age on a kitchen floor at 4:17 on a Tuesday — Remi looks at me. I do not smile. I also do not frown. I just meet the look. Remi’s face does something complicated. Then Remi picks up the cheese, separates it from the avocado with more care than I have ever used separating anything in my life, and puts both back on the tray. Remi eats the cheese. Remi eats the avocado. Scout reclaims the wooden spoon.

That is Being With.

Not a technique. Not a script. A one-year-old body learning, on a floor, that a feeling can pass through them without the adult in the room needing the feeling to be different than it is. I did not solve the avocado. I did not coach Remi through the avocado. I sat on the floor and let the avocado and the feeling be, together, for ninety seconds. Remi did the rest.

Every training I have ever given a teacher on this practice has, at its center, this shape. Not the words you say. Not the framework you cite. Not the curriculum on the shelf. Just—can you sit on the floor for ninety seconds without making the feeling someone else’s problem. Can your nervous system stay soft enough that a child can borrow it. Can you let the avocado be.

I can, on a Tuesday, about half the time. The other half I fix the avocado. I forgive myself. I sit down the next time.

That’s the whole thing, right there.

Skylar Belt is the co-founder of ConnectEd Circles, an early childhood consulting organization based in Asheville, NC.
