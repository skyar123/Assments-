The Mason Jar Lids (Part Two)

By Skylar Belt · Parent · Asheville, NC

A few months after we reorganized the living room, I found a lid in the bottom of the laundry basket. Silver, scuffed, a little bent where Remi had been gumming it for weeks last summer. I have no memory of putting it there. The washing machine had no comment.

The lids went away in the rearrangement. Not thrown out—never thrown out—put in a drawer in the kitchen with the lids that belonged on actual jars. I thought that was the end of them. The babies were cruising furniture by then. They had discovered the joy of pulling books off shelves one at a time and throwing them, solemn-faced, toward the center of the room. The lids had been replaced by gravity and by literature.

But here was a lid. In the laundry basket. And the next week, a different lid showed up behind the couch, and then a third in the diaper bag zipped into the same inner pocket I keep a pacifier in for emergencies. These lids were being moved. Someone was making decisions.

Scout, it turned out. Scout had not given up the lids. Scout had gone underground.

In the glossary I keep trying to keep short, I wrote that children outgrow transitional objects at their own pace. Some in a season. Some in years. Both are fine. I still believe that. I just didn’t know how sideways the pace could get. I thought outgrowing would look like forgetting. It looks, in Scout’s case, like stashing.

A mason jar lid does not fit anywhere obvious. It is not toy-shaped. It does not have a home on the shelf we built for toys. And that is—I think—the point. Scout has kept the ones that matter and hidden them in the corners of the apartment that belong to them. The laundry basket. The diaper bag. The pocket of Anja’s hoodie that is never washed because Anja never takes it off. The lids live where the parents live. Which is to say: with us, just out of sight.

I have decided to stop moving them back.

The research language for what Scout is doing would be something about object permanence and internal representation and the growing capacity to trust that a thing can exist when it is out of reach. Which is true. But I think the more honest version is simpler: the lids used to be a public practice, and now they are a private one. Scout doesn’t need to chew on them anymore. Scout needs to know they exist, in specific places, within specific distances of specific adults. That is a more sophisticated arrangement than the basket ever was.

Anja found a lid in the freezer. We don’t know how.

Tomorrow I will leave the lids where they land. I will resist the caretaker impulse to consolidate them in a bowl on the counter, which would be for me, not for Scout. I will notice that the living room looks, still, like a room built for one-year-olds who are becoming two-year-olds. And that somewhere, under a couch cushion, there is a silver disk that is doing the work of holding a feeling I can’t see yet.

That’s the whole thing, right there.

Skylar Belt is the co-founder of ConnectEd Circles, an early childhood consulting organization based in Asheville, NC. Skylar is a parent of twins and believes that small, everyday moments of connection are where the real work happens.
