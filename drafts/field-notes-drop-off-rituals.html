Drop-Off Rituals, and What They Actually Do

By Skylar Belt · Founder, ConnectEd Circles · Asheville, NC

A mother once told me, on the sidewalk outside a preschool I used to work at, that she had been doing drop-off wrong for two years and she was only just now realizing it. Her son was crying. She was crying quietly enough that you would have had to look at her twice to notice. She said, I keep sneaking out when he’s distracted. I thought that was easier. It’s not easier, is it.

It’s not easier.

Sneaking out is easier for the adult in the six seconds of the goodbye. What it does to the next twenty minutes of the child’s morning, and the next six months of their trust in transitions, is a different math entirely. A child who watches the caregiver leave can grieve the goodbye and move on. A child who turns around and discovers the caregiver has vanished has to hold two feelings at once—the missing of the person and the vigilance of not being told. Vigilance is expensive. It uses up the resources the child needed for block-building and friend-making and the lunch they haven’t eaten yet.

A ritual solves this. Not a complicated one. A short one, the same one, every single morning.

Here is the one we use with families, borrowed from the Circle of Security work and from years of standing in classroom doorways. You can change the words. You cannot change the shape.

One: I see you. I’m going now.

Two: Here’s what happens next. (A kiss. A high-five. A wave at the window. Whatever the thing is.)

Three: I will be back after snack (or nap, or story time, or the specific next event the child can feel the shape of).

Four: You go. You do not come back. You do not peek. You do not return for one more hug. The teacher holds the next four minutes.

That is it. Four seconds. Four steps. The same four steps on the snowy day and the rushed day and the day you forgot the lunch and the day your boss called while you were walking in.

What the ritual is doing is not what it looks like. It looks like a polite exchange between adults who both have somewhere to be. What it actually is: a scaffold that lets the child predict the future. And predictability is what regulation runs on. A child who can forecast the shape of the next sixty seconds can use those sixty seconds for something other than scanning for threat. That is the entire game.

Teachers: your job in the ritual is to be ready. Stand in the doorway. Use your body as the receiving surface. Have one hand for the child and one hand to wave the parent out. Do not perform reassurance on the parent—you are holding the child, not the parent’s guilt, and the parent will feel the difference. If the child cries, you narrate the crying. You’re sad. Mom is leaving. She’ll be back after snack. You do not try to stop the cry. You do not offer a sticker. You let the feeling be a feeling. This is Being With in its most compressed form.

Parents: your job in the ritual is to leave. That is a sentence I have had to say to a lot of loving, capable people who thought that leaving when it is hard is unkind. It is not unkind. It is the clearest possible message that you can handle this; I trust the teacher; I will come back. A child learns that sentence not from your words but from your feet walking away at the same moment every day.

One more thing. If a child asks for a transitional object—a piece of fabric from your shirt, a pebble from the driveway, the hair tie from your wrist—give it. Do not tell them it’s silly. It is not silly. It is a small technology the child has invented for carrying you in their pocket. Those technologies are older than we are.

The ritual will not fix every morning. It will not stop every cry. It will, over a season, build a shape the child can trust—and that is what we were after all along.

Skylar Belt is the co-founder of ConnectEd Circles, an early childhood consulting organization based in Asheville, NC. Skylar is a parent of twins and a Circle of Security facilitator.
